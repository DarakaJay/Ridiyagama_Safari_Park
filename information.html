Animal Profiles

Lion

Scientific Name: Panthera leo

Habitat: Grasslands, Savannas

Diet: Carnivorous

Size: Males can weigh between 330-550 pounds (150-250 kg), females between 260-400 pounds (120-180 kg)

Social Structure: Lions are the only truly social cats, living in groups called prides

Behavior: Lions are known for their cooperative hunting strategies, with females usually doing the majority of hunting

Conservation Status: Vulnerable, due to habitat loss, human-wildlife conflict, and poaching

Lifespan: Typically 10-14 years in the wild, longer in captivity

Elephant

Scientific Name: Loxodonta africana (African elephant) / Elephas maximus (Asian elephant)

Habitat: Savannas, grasslands, forests, and marshes (African elephants)/ Forests and grasslands (Asian elephants)

Diet: Herbivorous, feeding on grasses, fruits, bark, leaves, and roots

Size: African elephants can weigh up to 6,000-14,000 pounds (2,700-6,350 kg)/ Asian elephants are slightly smaller

Social Structure: Elephants live in matriarchal herds led by the oldest female, with strong family bonds

Behavior: Highly intelligent and social animals, known for their complex communication, memory, and use of tools

Conservation Status: African elephants are vulnerable, and Asian elephants are endangered due to habitat loss, poaching, and human-elephant conflict

Lifespan: Typically 60-70 years in the wild

Giraffe

Scientific Name: Giraffa camelopardalis

Habitat: Savannas, grasslands, and open woodlands across sub-Saharan Africa

Diet: Herbivorous, primarily feeding on leaves, fruits, and flowers, particularly from acacia trees

Size: Adult males can grow up to 18 feet (5.5 meters) tall, and females up to 14 feet / Males can weigh between 2,400-3,000 pounds (1,100-1,400 kg), while females typically weigh between 1,600-2,600 pounds (730-1,200 kg)(4.3 meters)

Social Structure: Live in loose herds that can range from a few individuals to over 20, with no permanent social bonds

Behavior: Known for their long necks, which they use to reach high foliage; they are also good runners, capable of speeds up to 35 miles per hour (56 km/h) in short bursts

Conservation Status: Vulnerable, primarily due to habitat loss and poaching

Lifespan: Typically 25 years in the wild

Zebra

Scientific Name: Equus zebra (Mountain zebra), Equus quagga (Plains zebra), Equus grevyi (Grévy's zebra)

Habitat:Grasslands, savannas, and open woodlands across sub-Saharan Africa

Diet: Herbivorous, feeding mainly on grasses, leaves, and shrubs

Size:Adult zebras typically stand between 4.5 to 5 feet (1.4 to 1.5 meters) at the shoulder / Range from 400 to 900 pounds (180 to 410 kg), depending on the species

Social Structure: Zebras are social animals, typically living in groups called harems, which consist of a stallion, several females, and their young

Behavior: Known for their distinctive black-and-white striped coats, which help with camouflage and may also regulate body temperature; they are excellent runners and can reach speeds of up to 65 km/h (40 mph)

Conservation Status: The Plains zebra is of least concern, while the Grevy's zebra is endangered and the Mountain zebra is vulnerable

Lifespan: Typically live between 20 to 30 years in the wild

Bengal Tiger

Scientific Name: Panthera tigris tigris

Habitat: Dense forests, grasslands, mangroves, and wetlands across India, Bangladesh, Bhutan, and Nepal

Diet: Carnivorous, primarily preying on ungulates like deer, wild boar, and sometimes smaller animals like rabbits

Size:Adult males typically weigh between 400 to 500 pounds (180 to 230 kg), and females are slightly smaller, weighing between 220 to 350 pounds (100 to 160 kg) / 8 to 10 feet (2.5 to 3 meters) from head to tail

Social Structure: Solitary, with males and females having separate territories

Behavior: Bengal tigers are excellent swimmers and are known for their stealth and power while hunting. They rely on their camouflage and patience to stalk and ambush prey.

Conservation Status: Endangered, due to habitat loss, poaching, and human-wildlife conflict

Lifespan: 10-15 years in the wild, up to 20 years in captivity

African buffalo

Scientific Name: Syncerus caffer

Habitat: Grasslands, savannas, and forests across sub-Saharan Africa

Diet: Herbivorous, primarily grazing on grasses, shrubs, and leaves

Size: Males can weigh between 1,100 to 2,200 pounds (500 to 1,000 kg), while females typically weigh between 600 to 1,100 pounds (270 to 500 kg)

Social Structure: African buffaloes are highly social animals, living in large herds that can range from a few dozen to several hundred individuals. Herds are usually led by the oldest females

Behavior: They are known for their strong herd mentality and cooperative behavior, often working together to protect one another from predators like lions. They are formidable opponents, capable of charging at high speeds to defend themselves

Conservation Status: Least Concern, although populations are declining in certain areas due to habitat loss, poaching, and disease

Lifespan: Typically live between 12 to 22 years in the wild

Ostrich

Scientific Name: Struthio camelus

Habitat: Savannas, deserts, and open woodlands across sub-Saharan Africa and parts of the Arabian Peninsula

Diet: Omnivorous, feeding on a variety of plant material, seeds, shrubs, and insects

Size: The ostrich is the largest living bird, standing 8 to 9 feet (2.5 to 2.7 meters) tall and weighing between 220 to 290 pounds (100 to 130 kg)

Social Structure: Ostriches are social animals that typically live in small groups or flocks, which can be led by a dominant male

Behavior: Known for their impressive running speed, ostriches can reach speeds of up to 45 mph (72 km/h). Despite their size, they are flightless but use their long legs for powerful running and kicking

Conservation Status: Least Concern, though populations are affected by habitat destruction and hunting

Lifespan: Typically live 30 to 40 years in the wild

Indian Blue Bull

Scientific Name: Boselaphus tragocamelus

Habitat: Grasslands, scrub forests, and open woodlands in India, Nepal, and parts of Pakistan

Diet: Herbivorous, feeding primarily on grasses, leaves, and shrubs

Size: Adult males typically weigh between 400 to 500 pounds (180 to 225 kg), while females weigh between 275 to 350 pounds (125 to 160 kg). They stand around 4.5 to 5 feet (1.4 to 1.5 meters) at the shoulder

Social Structure: Indian blue bulls are usually solitary or form small herds, especially in the case of females and young males. Dominant males may form territories

Behavior: Known for their agility and speed, they can run at speeds of up to 50 km/h (31 mph). They are also known for their distinct appearance, with a bluish-gray coat and white markings on the legs and underbelly

Conservation Status: Least Concern, though populations may be locally threatened by habitat loss and human-wildlife conflict

Lifespan: Typically live between 10 to 12 years in the wild

Spotted Deer

Scientific Name: Axis axis

Habitat: Forests, grasslands, and open woodlands across India, Sri Lanka, Nepal, Bhutan, and Bangladesh

Diet: Herbivorous, feeding primarily on grasses, fruits, leaves, and shrubs

Size: Adult males typically weigh between 130 to 210 pounds (60 to 95 kg), while females weigh between 100 to 150 pounds (45 to 70 kg). They stand about 3 feet (0.9 meters) at the shoulder

Social Structure: Spotted deer are social animals, typically living in herds led by a dominant male. Herds can range from a few individuals to over 50 animals

Behavior: Known for their characteristic spotted coat, which helps them blend into the dappled light of forests. They are agile and fast runners, capable of reaching speeds up to 40 km/h (25 mph) to escape predators

Conservation Status: Least Concern, though populations may be affected by habitat loss and hunting in certain regions

Lifespan: Typically live around 10 to 12 years in the wild

Arabian Oryx

Scientific Name: Oryx leucoryx

Habitat: Arid deserts, scrublands, and grasslands in the Arabian Peninsula, including Oman, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE

Diet: Herbivorous, primarily feeding on grasses, leaves, and fruits, and can survive with little water by obtaining moisture from the plants they consume

Size: Adults typically weigh between 330 to 440 pounds (150 to 200 kg) and stand about 3.3 to 3.9 feet (1 to 1.2 meters) at the shoulder

Social Structure: Arabian oryx live in herds, usually consisting of females and their young, while males tend to live alone or in bachelor groups, especially outside of the breeding season

Behavior: Known for their ability to tolerate extreme heat and drought conditions. They are typically crepuscular, meaning they are most active during the cooler parts of the day—early morning and late afternoon

Conservation Status: Vulnerable, due to habitat loss, hunting, and historical over-exploitation. However, the species has made a remarkable recovery thanks to conservation efforts and reintroduction programs

Lifespan: Typically live around 20 years in the wild

Blackbuck deer

Scientific Name:Antilope cervicapra

Habitat: Grasslands, open plains, and lightly wooded areas, primarily in India and Nepal

Diet: Herbivorous, feeding on grasses, herbs, fruits, and pods of shrubs

Size: Adults stand about 2.6 to 3.3 feet (80 to 100 cm) at the shoulder and weigh between 55 to 75 pounds (25 to 35 kg), with males being larger than females

Social Structure: Blackbucks live in herds typically comprising females and juveniles, while males often establish territories or form bachelor groups outside the mating season

Behavior: Known for their remarkable speed and agility, blackbucks can run at speeds of up to 50 miles per hour (80 km/h), helping them escape predators. Males are easily recognizable by their distinctive twisted horns

Conservation Status: Near Threatened, primarily due to habitat loss, hunting, and competition with livestock, though they are protected under wildlife conservation laws in India

Lifespan: Typically live between 10 to 15 years in the wild

Sambar Deer

Scientific Name:Rusa unicolor

Habitat: Dense forests, grasslands, and wetlands across South and Southeast Asia, including India, Sri Lanka, and parts of China and Indonesia

Diet: Herbivorous, feeding on grasses, leaves, fruits, bark, and aquatic plants

Size: Sambar deer are among the largest deer species, standing 3.9 to 5.3 feet (120 to 160 cm) tall at the shoulder and weighing between 220 to 770 pounds (100 to 350 kg), with males significantly larger than females

Social Structure: Generally solitary or found in small groups of 2-3 individuals; larger groups may form near water sources during the dry season

Behavior: Known for their loud alarm calls when they sense danger, often used to alert other animals about predators. Males grow large, rugged antlers used during the rutting season to compete for mates

Conservation Status: Vulnerable, due to habitat destruction, hunting, and predation. They are protected under conservation laws in several countries

Lifespan: Typically live between 12 to 20 years in the wild

Red Lechwe Deer

Scientific Name: Kobus leche

Habitat: Wetlands, floodplains, and marshy areas in southern Africa, particularly around the Okavango Delta, the Kafue Flats, and the Zambezi floodplains

Diet: Herbivorous, feeding primarily on aquatic grasses and other plants found in wetlands

Size: Adult males weigh between 150 to 260 pounds (70 to 120 kg) and stand about 3.3 to 3.9 feet (100 to 120 cm) at the shoulder; females are slightly smaller

Social Structure: Lechwes are social animals that live in herds, with males forming bachelor groups and females living in larger groups with their young

Behavior: Adapted to wetland environments, they have elongated hooves and water-repellent legs to move easily through flooded areas. They are excellent swimmers and often wade or run through water to escape predators

Conservation Status: Near Threatened, primarily due to habitat loss, hunting, and competition with livestock. Conservation efforts are in place in many parts of their range

Lifespan: Typically live 10 to 15 years in the wild

Donkey

Scientific Name: Equus asinus

Habitat: Native to arid and semi-arid regions of northern Africa; domesticated donkeys are found worldwide and thrive in deserts, grasslands, and mountainous areas

Diet: Herbivorous, primarily grazes on grasses, shrubs, and herbs, but can also consume bark, twigs, and coarse vegetation in arid environments

Size: Typically stands 3.5-5 feet (90-150 cm) at the shoulder. Weight ranges from 400-570 pounds (180-260 kg), depending on breed and diet

Social Structure: Domesticated donkeys are often kept in pairs or herds. In the wild, they are usually solitary or form small, loose groups, particularly during the breeding season

Behavior: Known for their surefootedness and adaptability, donkeys are highly intelligent and cautious animals. They have a strong sense of self-preservation and are often used as pack animals due to their endurance

Conservation Status: Critically Endangered due to habitat loss, hunting, and competition with livestock

Lifespan: 25-30 years on average, though some domesticated donkeys can live up to 40 years with proper care

Hippopotamus

Scientific Name: Hippopotamus amphibius

Habitat: Found in rivers, lakes, and wetlands across sub-Saharan Africa, typically in areas with abundant water to support their semi-aquatic lifestyle

Diet: Herbivorous, primarily grazes on short grasses and occasionally consumes aquatic vegetation, eating about 80 pounds (36 kg) of grass per night

Size: 10.8-16.5 feet (3.3-5 meters) length and 4.5-5.5 feet (1.4-1.65 meters) at the shoulder in height, Males can weigh 3,500-9,920 pounds (1,600-4,500 kg) and females typically weigh 2,900-3,300 pounds (1,300-1,500 kg)

Social Structure: Live in groups called pods, schools, or bloats, consisting of 10-30 individuals, led by a dominant male

Behavior: Hippopotamuses are semi-aquatic and spend most of their time in water to keep cool. They are highly territorial in water but less so on land. Despite their bulky appearance, they can run up to 19 miles per hour (30 km/h) on land over short distances

Conservation Status: Vulnerable, primarily due to habitat loss, poaching for meat and ivory (from their teeth), and human-wildlife conflicts

Lifespan: Typically 40-50 years in the wild, in captivity they can live slightly longer with proper care

Wild Boar

Scientific Name: Sus scrofa

Habitat: Found in a wide range of environments, including forests, grasslands, wetlands, and agricultural areas across Europe, Asia, North Africa, and introduced regions such as the Americas and Oceania

Diet: Omnivorous; diet includes roots, fruits, seeds, tubers, small animals, eggs, and carrion

Size: Adults typically range from 3-6 feet (90-180 cm) in length and 20-40 inches (50-100 cm) in height at the shoulder. Males can weigh between 150-330 pounds (70-150 kg), while females are slightly smaller, averaging 100-200 pounds (45-90 kg)

Social Structure: Females and their offspring live in matriarchal groups called sounders, while mature males are generally solitary except during mating season

Behavior: Known for their adaptability and intelligence, wild boars are excellent foragers and are capable swimmers. They are primarily nocturnal but can be active during the day in undisturbed areas

Conservation Status: Least Concern, due to their wide distribution and adaptability, but populations are managed in many regions to prevent overpopulation and human-wildlife conflicts

Lifespan: Typically 10-14 years in the wild, although many do not survive past 4-5 years due to hunting and predation

Lesser Kudu

Scientific Name: Tragelaphus imberbis

Habitat: Found in dry savannas, woodlands, and scrublands of eastern Africa, particularly in Ethiopia, Somalia, Kenya, and Tanzania. Prefers dense bush and thickets for cover

Diet: Herbivorous browser; feeds on leaves, twigs, shoots, fruits, flowers, and occasionally grasses. Forages primarily during dawn and dusk

Size: Adults stand 3.3-4.1 feet (1-1.25 meters) at the shoulder. Males weigh 200-260 pounds (90-120 kg), while females weigh 110-160 pounds (50-73 kg)

Social Structure: Solitary or found in small groups. Females and their young form groups of 2-4 individuals, while males are usually solitary except during mating season

Behavior: Known for their agility and ability to leap over 8 feet (2.5 meters) to escape predators. They are primarily nocturnal and crepuscular, hiding in dense vegetation during the day to avoid detection

Conservation Status: Near Threatened, due to habitat destruction, poaching, and competition with livestock for resources

Lifespan:Typically 8-10 years in the wild

FAQs

  • What are the park's opening hours?
    The park is open daily from 6:30 AM to 5:30 PM.
  • Do I need to book tickets in advance?
    It is recommended to book tickets online in advance to avoid waiting in lines.
  • Are there guided tours available?
    Yes, we offer guided tours throughout the day. You can book a guide when you purchase your ticket.
  • Can I bring food into the park?
    Food and drinks from outside are not allowed. However, there are food stalls available inside the park.
  • Is there parking available at the park?
    Yes, there is ample parking space available for visitors.
